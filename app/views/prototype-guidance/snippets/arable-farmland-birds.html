Guidance

Arable farmland birds

This management is targeted to benefit the following group of birds: grey partridge, linnet, reed bunting, skylark, barn owl, kestrel, yellowhammer, tree sparrow and yellow wagtail.

Following the information given in this guidance will benefit a broad range of declining farmland bird species found on any arable farmland. See, the guidance for managing non-arable areas for farmland birds.

The needs of ‘bespoke species’ can be met by bespoke tailoring of management techniques. If you would like to target a bespoke species choose the relevant guidance: lapwing, stone curlew, turtle dove, cirl bunting and corn bunting.

To target and plan your management you need to know which of these species are likely to be present on your holding, or in the surrounding landscape. You can find this information from: MAP/Websites/Magic/ Red List Revival.

Once you know which species you are targeting, you can decide the level of management you want to apply. If you decide to target a ‘bespoke species’ then you should use the reverse or recover levels of management. These species require specialised land management and a bigger commitment. For each level of management, you need to provide the ‘essential resources’ on a percentage of the arable land on the holding. These percentages are given below:

Farmland Bird Levels of Management

Stabilise

Provide the right resources to stabilise the existing bird population on your farm

Reverse

Provide the right resources to begin to turn things aroundto reverse the decline of birds on your farm

Recover

Provide the right resources to secure a sustainable recovery of the population of birds on your farm

How to provide the essential resources for farmland birds

You can use a range of land management techniques to provide the essential resources required by farmland birds. The following tables show the types of land management that provide each essential resource for farmland birds. To understand how to use these land management techniques to provide the essential resources, read the guidance available for each technique. This includes guidance on combining and locating management techniques to maximise their benefit for farmland birds.

Nesting habits for farmland birds
Image

Choose one or more of the following techniques:

  • Management of hedgerows
  • Management of ditches
  • Buffer strips
  • Buffer strips
  • Beetle banks
  • Wildlife boxes - of different sizes
  • Skylark plots
  • Nesting plots for lawing and stone curlew

Different bird species have different preferences for where they choose to nest. For example, tree sparrows nest in holes in trees or farm buildings, whereas skylarks nest on the ground in open areas. You can meet the needs of more bird species by providing a range of different types of nesting habitat.

Spring food - insect rich foraging for chick food
Image

Choose two of the following techniques:

  • Nectar flower mix
  • Flower-rich margins and plots
  • Legume fallow
  • Cultivated areas for arable plans
  • Buffer strips

Creating a range of these habitats will provide farmland birds with a greater choice of feeding habitats. It will also maintain a source of food for chicks throughout the breeding season, as different plants flower at different times.

Most territorial farmland birds typically forage up to 250m from the nest site. Spread foraging habitats around the holding so that more breeding birds have access to food for their chicks.

Winter food
Image

Choose one or more the following techniques in addition to winter bird food plots:

  • Overwinter stubble
  • Brassica fodder crops
  • Whole crop cereals
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds

Create a range of these habitats to provide a greater choice of seed types and access. This will also maintain seed sources from autumn until spring, helping birds survive over winter when food is scarce. To feed as many birds as possible these habitats should be between 500m and 1000m apart.

Between New Year and spring is known as the ‘hungry gap’. This is because there is a lack of natural food available for birds during this period. Achieving the Reverse and Recover management levels will help your farmland birds survive the winter.

If you wish to target a particular species, the tables below give guidance on how best to provide the essential resources for each species. Read the highlighted guidance to understand how to meet the needs of each species.

Grey partridge

Requirements

Tussocky grass for nesting. Insect-rich field margins and headlands for chick-rearing. Seed-rich feeding habitat all year.

Management techniques
  • Unharvested conservation headlands
  • Buffer strips
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Beetle banks
  • Winter bird food - biennial mixes
  • Unharvested ryegrass
  • Overwinter stubble
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds

Additional information

 These techniques are most effective whenthere isactive game keeping on the hold
Linnet

Requirements

Hedgerows and scrub for nesting and abundant, accessible year-round supplies of small and medium-sized seeds

Management techniques
  • Management of hedgerows
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Winter bird food - biennial mixes containing oil seeds
  • Overwinter stubble
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds

Additional information

 Highest densities in arable landscapes are typically associated with fields of oil-seed rape, which is now a vital food source once the seeds become available
Reed bunting

Requirements

On farmland, nests are mainly in marginal vegetation, especially along ditches and in oil-seed rape. Chicks are fed on insects but full-grown birds eat seeds.

Management techniques
  • Buffer strips
  • Management of ditches
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Overwinter Stubble
  • Brassica fodder crops
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds
  • Unharvested ryegrass

Additional information

 Highest densities in arable landscapes typically occur where oil-seed rape is grown in areas with extensive ditch networks. Spraying-off, as opposed to swathing of oil-seed rape to desiccate the crop enables additional later nests to be successful and so should be encouraged.
Skylarks

Requirements

In- field nester in arable or grass swards of 20–50cm, but the sward must be open to allow access to the ground for foraging and nesting. Chicks are fed on invertebrates and full-grown birds feed on seeds and green plant material.

Management techniques
  • Skylark plots in fields of winter wheat, at a minimum density of 2 per ha of crop
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Winter bird food - annual, cereal-based mixtures but must be placed away from hedgerows. Therefore these plots are not generally compatible with needs of other species.
  • Overwinter stubble
  • Brassica fodder crops
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds
  • Unharvested ryegrass

Additional information

 Open, typically mixed farming, landscapes with a variety of crop types are best, as these provide the most nesting opportunities and are also likely to have stubble fields. Nests in silage fields are often destroyed during mowing. Skylarks will also benefit from nesting plots targeted at lapwing and stone curlew.
Barn owl

Requirements

Nests in buildings but increasingly in provided, purposely-designed nest boxes. Hunts small mammals in rough grassland. Ideally thick, tussocky grass with a deep litter-layer, which is ideal habitat for their main prey, field voles.

Management techniques
  • Lage wildlife box (in pairs, to provide nesting and roosting sites, and for multiple broods)
  • Buffer strips
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Beetle banks
  • Management of ditches
  • Management of tussocky grassland
  • Take field corners out of management

Additional information

 Ensure management techniques are located at least 1km from the nearest motorway, dual carriageway or similar fast, open-plan road. Research suggests that the ideal quantity of rough grass in the landscape is 2.5 to 3.7% in pastoral, 1.4 to 2% in mixed, and 1.1 to 1.7% in arable.
Kestrel

Requirements

Nests in or on buildings, on natural cliffs and, increasingly, in nest boxes. Hunts small mammals, birds and large invertebrates.

Management techniques
  • Large wildlife box
  • Buffer strips
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Beetle banks
  • Take field corners out of management

Additional information

 Habitats should be managed to provide areas of long and short grass in close proximity - to provide conditions suitable for their small mammal prey and access to them.
Yellowhammer

Requirements

Nests on or close to the ground in ditch vegetation or at the base of hedgerows and scrub. They prefer hedgerows that have a wide, uncut grass margin or ditch. Chicks are fed on invertebrates and full-grown birds eat seeds.

Management techniques
  • Management of hedgerows
  • Buffer strips
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Take field corners out of management
  • Winter bird food - annual, cereal-based mixtures
  • Unharvested ryegrass
  • Overwinter stubble
  • Brassica fodder crops
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds
Tree sparrow

Requirements

Nests in colonies in holes in trees, farm buildings and increasingly nest boxes. Chicks are fed on insects, often collected from waterside habitats, as well as farmland. Full-grown birds eat seeds.

Management techniques
  • Small wildlife box
  • Buffer strips
  • Flower rich margins and plots
  • Management of ponds
  • Management of ditches
  • Winter bird food - annual, cereal-based mixtures
  • Overwinter stubble
  • Supplementary winter feeding for farmland birds

Additional information

 Evidence suggests that nest boxes are most successful where abundant seed food is provided, ideally year-round. In combination with access to waterside or wetland or other invertebrate-rich habitat to provide an abundant source of chick food.
Yellow wagtail

Requirements

Nests in arable crops and feeds on invertebrates in a variety of locations where birds are able to catch them, on the ground or in flight.

Management techniques

 Research is still in progress to identify the most beneficial management techniques for yellow wagtails. Whilst birds can have two nesting attempts in winter wheat, the second nesting attempt is often in vegetable crops, especially potatoes, where these are available. Nests in field beans are often predated. Damper, more penetrable soils have higher densities of birds, which is probably because there are more insects in these areas. Wet ditches and tracks are favoured foraging habitats, and birds use fallow plots for feeding
