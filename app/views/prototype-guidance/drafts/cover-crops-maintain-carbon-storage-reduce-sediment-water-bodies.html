A5: Use cover crops to maintain carbon storage and reduce sediment in water bodies

Find out what you need to do to maintain carbon storage and reduce sediment in water bodies by planting cover crops.

Published 26 February 2020
From: Natural England

Contents

  1. Benefits of doing this action
  2. How to plant and manage cover crops
  3. Where you cannot do this action
  4. Use this action for Environmental Land Management (ELM)

Cover crops can be grown to protect or improve soils. They are also known as ‘non-cash’ crops. Types of cover crops include hairy vetch, cereal rye and red clover.

Cover crops can be used to:

  • suppress weeds
  • prevent soil erosion and sediment leaching
  • build and maintain soil fertility
  • control pests and diseases

Benefits of doing this action

Cover crops help to reduce water and air pollution by improving:

  • soil quality
  • soil stability
  • carbon storage

Planting cover crops can also work to increase organic matter in soils to reduce wind erosion.

How to plan and manage cover crops

Choose the right location

This action will give the best results if you plant cover crops on land that is at moderate or high risk of surface run-off or soil erosion. Light sandy soils in nitrate vulnerable areas are most at risk.

If you are planting next to a watercourse you should consider creating grass buffer strips. They will help to capture any sediment or organic materials carried in runoff water. Find out more about creating grass buffer strips.

Choose the right cover crop for your land and soil type

You can use either a mix or a single crop of:

  • seeds, such as rye, vetch or mustard
  • other crops, such as ryegrass or tillage radish

Your choice of crop should depend on:

  • what other crops have been grown in the rotation
  • what time of year you are planting
  • the herbicide you use
  • the rates of application for the previous crop

How to manage your cover crop

To manage your cover crop you may want to:

  • remove areas of soil compaction (but do not subsoil on archaeological features)
  • establish by drilling or broadcasting
  • sow at a suitable seed rate to provide a dense cover and protect from soil erosion

This guidance describes an action that can be used as part of an ELM agreement.
