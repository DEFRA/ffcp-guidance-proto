Thriving arable plants: cultivated margins and plots

Find out how to grow rare and declining wild plants by cultivating arable land.

Published 26 February 2020
From: Natural England

About arable plants

Arable plants are rare wild plants that grow on land that is used, or is suitable for, growing crops. They include species such as field madder, dwarf spurge and corn buttercup.

You can plough fields (known as plots) and edges of fields (called margins) in a way that encourages arable plants to grow.

How do I find out if I have arable plants on my land?

Arable plants can be found on unfertilised land, such as:

  • the outer edges of a field of broad-leaved crops
  • on tracks
  • at the bases of telegraph poles

Most rare arable plants are found in the east and south-west of England. Even if you have not seen them, it’s possible that seeds are underground. Find out if you live in a rare arable plant ‘hotspot’ on the Plantlife website. Local record centres are also a good source of information.

Benefits of cultivating land for arable plants

By creating cultivated areas, you will be giving rare and declining plants space to grow, which will:

  • stop them from disappearing from our landscape altogether
  • supply nectar and pollen for pollinating insects
  • provide food and shelter for insects and farmland birds such as turtle doves
  • add colour to the landscape

Consider also providing winter bird food and nesting plots for ground nesting birds as you will then begiving farmland birds and insects everything they need forthe whole year.

How to cultivate land for arable plants

You can create cultivated areas as field margins or larger plots on a part or whole field basis. Big plots are best located in places where you know you have several species of arable plants across a large area.

Avoid applying fertiliser as this will encourage competition from weeds. Having a buffer between your cultivated area and arable crop can help reduce fertiliser drift.

Where to cultivate

If you’ve seen arable plants, you should cultivate the areas you have found them. Keep the plot in the same place (a non rotational plot) unless you have problems with weeds or crop diseases, when rotating the plot may be better.

If you know what plants you’re trying to grow, but haven’t seen them, use the guidance on preferred soil types to locate your plots on the right type of soil.

If you're in an arable plant 'hotspot' but unsure what plants you'll be able to grow, you should locate your plot in:

  • open, sunny locations
  • the outermost 6 metres of your fields, next to track and boundaries
  • areas at the top of slopes as the soil is often shallow, and the subsoil less fertile and productive
  • fields that have a long history of cultivation as some arable plant seeds can survive for long periods in the soil

You should avoid:

  • cool, north-facing shaded locations, as most seeds will not germinate
  • land with perennial and arable grass weed problems as these will stop arable plants from growing

When you’re trying to grow plants you haven’t seen on your land before, you should move your plot from time to time and return the land to conventional cropping. This will increase your chances of:

  • discovering new arable plants
  • controlling weeds and crop diseases

When to cultivate

If you know what plants you’re trying to grow, read the guidance on managing your land for arable plants to find out the best times to start and end cultivation.

If you know you have plants that germinate in the spring and the autumn, then it’s best to vary the timing of cultivation between different plots each year. This should help to grow the widest mix of species.

If you don’t know what arable plants you have, then varying the cultivation between spring and autumn can encourage a wider range of species.

If you don’t know what plants you have, you can leave the cultivated areas in place until the end of August. This will allow most species to complete their life cycle and set seed.

What depth to cultivate to

Read the guidance on managing your land for arable plants to find out the best depth to cultivate for the plant you’re trying to grow.

If you don’t know which plant species you have, randomly vary the cultivation depth between 5cm and 15 cm every 2 or 3 years. This should encourage the greatest number of species.

How to control weeds

Weeds might grow in your cultivated area, especially in the first year. Over time, as nutrient levels decrease, so will the weeds.

Read the guidance on the best weed control methods.

What a cultivated margin or plot should look like

You will know you’ve been successful when you:

  • see arable plants and seeds every year from mid-April to the end of August
  • open patches with some bare areas and shorter vegetation, giving birds easy access to seeds on the ground
  • bees, wasps, hoverflies and butterflies foraging for pollen and nectar
