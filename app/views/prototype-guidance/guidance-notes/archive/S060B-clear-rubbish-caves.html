Guidance

S060B: Remove dumped rubbish from protected geological sites below ground

How land managers can clear dumped rubbish from protected geological sites, like SSSIs, that are below the ground, and what surveys or licences you need.

Published 8 June 2020
From: Natural England

Contents

Sites of special scientific interest (SSSIs), and local geological sites, below the ground

Geological SSSIs and local geological sites are protected.

Below-ground geological features that make up these sites can include:

  • caves
  • gorges and sinkholes leading to caves
  • buried minerals, sediments and fossils

These features can appear on any land where quarries, mines or cave systems exist, but caves are more common in limestone areas.

If you’re not sure whether you have a protected site on your land, search for protected areas of countryside.

Dumped rubbish

Dumped rubbish means modern materials, like:

  • disused farm machinery
  • plastic waste
  • building rubble

Dumped rubbish can:

  • cause damage to sites
  • prevent access to sites for scientific and educational study
  • prevent use of sites for recreation, like caving or potholing
  • pollute land and water
  • harm wildlife
You must not remove items that have archaeological or geological value. For example, sinkholes can contain remains of ancient human activity, like animal bones.

Before you remove dumped rubbish

Get a survey of the site’s geology

You’ll need to get a geological survey of the site. The survey will advise you on the safest way to clear the rubbish.

If you need to remove rubbish from underground you’ll also need a geotechnical survey. This is to make sure the structure of the site is safe.

The surveyor will tell you if you need more specialist advice from, for example:

  • bat conservationists
  • archaeologists

To arrange a survey, contact …[?].

Get a wildlife survey

You’ll need to get a wildlife survey to find out if there are protected species using the feature.

You’ll need a licensed surveyor to do this.

Protected species include:

  • bats(
  • badgers)
  • birds

The surveyor will visit at a time when species will be present. For example, breeding bats are found in summer, while hibernating bats are found in winter.

If there are protected species present, you’ll need a licence before you start the work.

Check who you need to tell

If the rubbish is on an SSSI you must tell Natural England. You can contact Natural England by email: ProtectedSites@naturalengland.org.uk

If the site is on or near a scheduled monument you must tell Historic England before you remove the rubbish.

! Warning You could get a fine or go to prison if you damage a protected site or monument.

To remove large amounts of material you might also need planning permission. Contact your [local council’s](https://www.gov.uk/find-local-council) planning department to find out.
